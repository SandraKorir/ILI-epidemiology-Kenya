Introduction

This analysis explores trends in Influenza-Like Illnesses (ILI) across various counties in Kenya in the years 2023-2024, in the epidemiological weeks 1-5.
The data provided includes: records on ILI percentages, the population for different age groups, and county-specific details. The main objective is to evaluate temporal and county-specific ILI trends and interpret the findings to inform public health decisions. Specifically, the aim is to compute the mean ILI percentage per county per year, analyze the weekly trend of ILI, calculate incidence rates, and compare ILI percentages across counties. Results from this analysis will help identify patterns and inform potential public health responses.

The ILI weekly trend plot across counties from epi-weeks 1-5 shows relatively stable levels of the illnesses at the beginning of the year.

Influenza-Like Illnesses seemed to have increased and peaked in epi-weeks 2 and 4. In epi-week 2, ILI cases rose in most counties, were the highest in Kakamega, but had dropped in Nakuru and Mombasa. In epi-week 4, ILI cases peaked in Machakos and Nairobi, but dropped in Kisumu and Mombasa.

This seasonal patterns suggest localized outbreaks rather than major seasonal changes across the counties. ILI seasons vary across counties. Nonetheless, most counties have surges in epi-weeks 2 (mid-January) and 4 (late January and early February).

# A one-way ANOVA
Kakamega_data = filtered_data[filtered_data['county'] == 'Kakamega']['ili_percentage']
Kisumu_data = filtered_data[filtered_data['county'] == 'Kisumu']['ili_percentage']
Nakuru_data = filtered_data[filtered_data['county'] == 'Nakuru']['ili_percentage']

# Combining data for ANOVA
data_to_compare = [Kakamega_data, Kisumu_data, Nakuru_data]

# Performing ANOVA
f_statistic, p_value = stats.f_oneway(*data_to_compare)

# Results
print("F-statistic:", f_statistic)
print("P-value:", p_value)

Findings

According to the ILI weekly trend plot,Influenza-Like Ilnesses peaked in epi-weeks 2 and 4. Though the peaks are generalized, some counties experienced sharper increases, and others, drops even when others had peaks. The fact that ILI seasons varies across counties in these weeks highlights the need to conduct surveillance continuously, even during traditionally low transmission periods

On comparing ILI Incidence rates per 100,000 population across the selected counties (Kakamega, Kisumu, and Nakuru), the result is that Kakamega had the highest mean incident rate, followed by Nakuru and then Kisumu. There is a slight regional variation in disease burden when you look at the incidence numbers. However, An ANOVA test, where F-statistic is 0.7770842041341016 and P-value is 0.46210263265067264, shows that there is no statistically significant difference in ILI percentages across the three counties. This means that the average burden of ILI was relatively similar across these three counties during the study period.

These trends imply early-year seasonality in ILI transmission across the sampled counties. Certain counties may require targeted intervention. To help reduce the ILI burden, public health responses could include: strengthened and continuous ILI surveillance, more preventive campaings, and public awareness; especially before anticipated peak weeks and in more affected regions like Kakamega.
